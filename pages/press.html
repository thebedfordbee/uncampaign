A $49.99 Campaign Building Civic Tools Instead of Buying Attention

Don Scott’s campaign for Bedford Town Supervisor is an intentionally unconventional experiment in local democracy: no traditional fundraising, no mailers, no paid consultants, no robocalls, no door knocking, and no paid ad dependence.

Instead, the campaign is building public-facing civic tools, openly using AI, publishing its spending, and documenting a different way to run for local office.

Bedford deserves a choice. This campaign is trying to prove that choice does not have to be expensive.

Why This Is a Story

Most campaigns ask for money. This one is asking a different question.

Local campaigns usually follow a familiar script: raise money, hire consultants, print signs, send mailers, knock on doors, buy ads, and try to look bigger than they are.

This campaign is deliberately doing the opposite.

It is built around a simpler civic question: should Bedford’s Town Supervisor election offer voters a real choice?

  1. 1 A Republican running in a heavily Democratic town without trying to nationalize the race.
  2. 2 A down-ballot local campaign designed around participation, not pressure.
  3. 3 A public spend target of $49.99 or less.
  4. 4 Open use of AI in campaign planning, writing, civic tools, and voter education.
  5. 5 A set of public tools that invite residents to compare ideas, not just react to slogans.
  6. 6 A campaign model transparent enough to be studied, copied, challenged, or improved.

It is a local campaign, but the question behind it is larger: how much money does democracy actually need?

The Simple Version

A local campaign trying to earn attention by being useful.

The UNCAMPAIGN is not trying to simulate momentum with fundraising emails, lawn signs, paid ads, or consultant language. It is trying to earn attention by building things Bedford residents can actually use.

“We are not treating residents as targets, donors, or data points. We are inviting them to think with us.”

That means structured issue pages, public ranking tools, a resident-centered Roundtable, transparent AI workflows, and a campaign budget that remains small enough to understand without a finance degree.

What We Built

Civic tools instead of campaign theater.

The campaign’s central bet is that residents will pay more attention to useful tools than to traditional campaign noise. Each tool is designed to make local politics easier to understand, easier to question, and easier to participate in.

The Bedford Roundtable

The Bedford Roundtable is a structured public conversation format for local issues. Instead of presenting one campaign talking point, it shows how different Bedford residents might reasonably see the same problem. It is designed to surface tradeoffs, tensions, shared concerns, and practical middle ground.

Press angleA local campaign using AI-assisted civic simulation to model better public conversation.

Ideas Ranking + Matchups

The ideas system lets residents compare possible town priorities through rankings and head-to-head matchups. It is a low-cost way to learn what people actually value when they have to choose between competing ideas. This is different from a petition, a Facebook comment thread, or a traditional poll. It asks residents to make tradeoffs.

Press angleA local campaign turning issue discovery into an interactive civic product.

Ask Don

Ask Don is an AI-assisted question tool built from Don’s own answers, language, and judgment. It is designed to help residents explore where Don stands, what he knows, what he does not know yet, and what he believes belongs in the Town Supervisor’s lane. It does not replace Don. It makes his thinking easier to access.

Press angleA transparent use of AI for candidate accessibility, with human-reviewed source material.

The Rules

The Rules page explains what this campaign will not do: no fundraising machine, no door knocking, no mailers, no robocalls, no pressure tactics, no fake urgency, and no pretending this is normal. The rules are not a gimmick. They are the campaign’s operating system.

Public Spending Transparency

Every dollar spent by the campaign is shown publicly. The target is $49.99 or less. That number is intentionally small. The point is not that every campaign can or should spend this little. The point is to test how much civic participation can be created before money becomes the answer.

Press angleA local candidate treating campaign finance transparency as a live product, not a compliance footnote.

AI Methods in Public

The campaign openly explains where AI helps, where it does not, and how residents can use similar tools themselves. AI is not being used to fake support, impersonate people, or manufacture endorsements. It is being used to reduce cost, organize information, draft materials, test ideas, and make civic participation easier to access.

Press angleA local campaign treating AI literacy as part of civic participation.

AI, Without the Magic Trick

The AI story is not that the campaign uses AI. It is what AI makes possible at this scale.

A local campaign that once would have needed designers, copywriters, data staff, web developers, consultants, pollsters, and a serious fundraising operation can now build credible public tools with almost no money. That changes the economics of participation.

This campaign is not using AI to make politics less human. It is using AI to remove some of the cost and friction that keep ordinary people from participating in local politics at all.

The campaign is also teaching as it goes. When AI helps draft copy, structure a tool, summarize resident priorities, or build a page, the campaign is willing to say so. The goal is not to make AI seem mysterious. The goal is to make civic technology feel reachable.

AI is the tool. Bedford is the subject. Don is the candidate.

Possible Story Angles

Several stories are happening at once.

For reporters, editors, researchers, and civic technologists, this campaign can be understood through several lenses.

The campaign is testing whether modern tools can replace parts of the traditional campaign apparatus while keeping the process transparent and human-reviewed.

The UNCAMPAIGN is trying to generate civic engagement through public products: issue explainers, rankings, matchups, AI-assisted Q&A, and structured local conversation.

There is no pressure ladder here. No donor funnel. No door-knocking script. The campaign is built around availability and resident choice.

The campaign publishes spending as part of the message. The budget is not hidden behind compliance reports. It is part of the argument.

Bedford’s Town Supervisor race is local, but the broader dynamics are familiar: nationalized politics, expensive campaigns, low trust, and residents tired of being marketed to.

The campaign is not claiming to have solved local democracy. It is making a testable argument: more people might participate if campaigns were more transparent, less intrusive, and more useful.

The Difference

This is not a protest campaign. It is a proof-of-concept.

The campaign is not built around outrage. It is not built around personal attacks. It is not asking Bedford residents to join a movement, pick a fight, or pretend national politics belongs in every local conversation.

It is built around a quieter premise: local democracy works better when residents have choices, information, and low-friction ways to participate.

The campaign may win, lose, or simply prove that a different model is possible. But the experiment itself is the story: a local candidate using transparency, restraint, and civic technology to make a buried race harder to ignore.
