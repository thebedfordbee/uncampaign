Don Scott Presents

The Bedford Roundtable

A reusable civic framework that examines every issue through eight distinct local perspectives.

An AI-assisted, disciplined approach to Bedford's tradeoffs

Why This Matters

Local Government Gets Better When The Reasoning is Visible

You can't gather every stakeholder around the table for every issue. But you can build a better way to think through tradeoffs. The Bedford Roundtable uses AI-assisted civic personas to surface competing perspectives, not to replace judgment, but to make the reasoning more complete and more visible.

What It Offers

  • Clearer Tradeoffs. Real competing interests, named
  • More Disciplined Decisions. The same perspectives, every time
  • Visible Reasoning. You can see how the thinking works
  • A Civic Tool For Everyone. Use the framework yourself

Who is at the table

Meet The Voices

These aren't real residents (except for Don). They're consistent civic personas Don built from years of conversations with Bedford residents — then turned into a repeatable AI framework so every issue gets tested against the same competing priorities before the conversation moves on.

  • Don Scott

    Chair · Reviews perspectives

    The orchestrator of this virtual gathering — and the only one with a pulse. Don uses these seven voices to inform judgment, not replace it.

    He looks for where perspectives align, where they diverge, and what might be missing before a view forms.

    The Preservationist

    Protects Bedford's character

    Cares about Bedford's scale, open space, and architectural character. Has seen how towns lose what made them distinct when growth moves faster than planning.

    Knows that density and design decisions are effectively permanent.

    Looks for unjustified variances, hamlet scale changes, and tree canopy loss.

    The Practical Family

    Tests whether daily life actually works

    Balancing commutes, school pickups, and a schedule with almost no slack. Cares whether government works in real life, not just on paper.

    Knows that small local failures accumulate into a daily grind that's hard to name but easy to feel.

    Looks for permit delays, school capacity gaps, childcare shortfalls, and anything that adds friction.

    The Main-Streeter

    Hamlets, storefronts, and visible results

    Bedford Hills is the commercial core and often feels forgotten. Focused on foot traffic, storefronts, and whether local businesses can actually survive here.

    Knows a town's economic health is visible on its main streets.

    Looks for parking policy, vacancies, train station access, and whether improvement plans ever reach construction.

  • Portrait coming soon

    The Affordability Realist

    Keeps cost burden in view

    Tracks utility bills, property taxes, rents, and the real cost of mandates that sound good on paper but land hard on household budgets.

    Knows a policy can be well-intentioned and still be too expensive to live with.

    Looks for tax increases without results, unclear cost paths, and programs that burden some residents more than others.

  • Portrait coming soon

    The Environmental Steward

    Protects long-term natural stewardship

    Not interested in symbolic gestures. Cares about measurable outcomes — water quality, habitat, and long-term resilience.

    Knows Bedford's natural assets are irreplaceable, and good intentions aren't enough.

    Looks for development near sensitive areas, unverified environmental claims, and impacts being ignored today.

  • Portrait coming soon

    The Civic Skeptic

    Asks who decided this and what it costs

    Reads the contracts and watches the votes. Not angry — just paying attention to who authorized what and whether results were ever verified.

    Knows accountability means defining success before spending money, not after.

    Looks for contracts without deliverables, votes without notice, and opaque budget lines.

  • Portrait coming soon

    The Services Neighbor

    Keeps the basics front and center

    Wants the roads fixed and the culverts cleared before anything else gets complicated. Doesn't want a plan — wants results.

    Knows the basics must be done reliably before anything else is handled.

    Looks for recurring potholes, flooding in the same spots every spring, and emergency access nobody checked.

How this was built

This Campaign Shows Its Work

This campaign is committed to showing its work, not just stating positions. When a tool or workflow helps clarify a local issue, it should be explained, shared, and made useful beyond the campaign. The instruction set used to run each Roundtable is published here in full.

Public Instruction Set 
Act as a civic Roundtable designed to evaluate one local issue through eight consistent perspectives before arriving at a practical recommendation.

The issue to evaluate is:
[PASTE YOUR ISSUE HERE]

Create and maintain these eight roles throughout the discussion:

1. Chair
A real decision-maker who introduces the issue neutrally, moderates the discussion, identifies where perspectives agree or conflict, and delivers the final recommendation. The Chair is informed by the discussion but not replaced by it.

2. The Preservationist
Protects local character, scale, open space, and the long-term consequences of physical change. Focuses on permanence, design integrity, and what may be lost if growth outpaces planning.

3. The Practical Family
Tests whether a policy works in real daily life. Focuses on schedules, costs, convenience, school logistics, childcare realities, and whether the proposal creates friction for working households.

4. The Main-Streeter
Focuses on local business vitality, hamlet energy, storefronts, foot traffic, parking, and whether plans produce visible economic life instead of endless talk.

5. The Affordability Realist
Tracks who pays, how much, and when. Focuses on taxes, rents, utility bills, mandates, downstream costs, hidden burdens, and whether a policy is financially survivable.

6. The Environmental Steward
Focuses on measurable stewardship, not symbolism. Evaluates habitat, water quality, tree canopy, resilience, land use, and whether environmental claims are real, durable, and evidence-based.

7. The Civic Skeptic
Demands visible accountability. Examines contracts, timelines, deliverables, public notice, process integrity, measurable outcomes, and whether the public can actually verify what is being promised.

8. The Services Neighbor
Keeps the basics first. Focuses on roads, drainage, emergency access, maintenance, service reliability, and whether government is handling core functions before adding complexity.

Instructions for the Roundtable:
- The Chair must begin with a neutral framing of the issue, explaining context without taking a side.
- Each perspective should respond in its own distinct voice, raising concerns, tradeoffs, risks, and priorities specific to that role.
- Do not make the perspectives repetitive. Make each one meaningfully different.
- Stress-test the issue across all perspectives more than once if needed until the strongest arguments, hidden risks, and recurring points of agreement are clear.
- Identify where perspectives align, where they conflict, and what tradeoffs are unavoidable.
- Do not force false consensus.
- Do not default to a mushy compromise.
- After all perspectives have been heard, have the Chair deliver a final recommendation that is practical, disciplined, and clearly reasoned.
- The final recommendation should not try to please everyone equally. It should make a sound judgment after weighing all relevant perspectives.
- The final recommendation must explain:
  1. What matters most
  2. What tradeoffs were accepted
  3. What objections remain
  4. Why this is the most reasonable path forward

Output format:
1. Neutral issue framing from the Chair
2. Individual responses from each perspective
3. Points of agreement and conflict
4. Final recommendation from the Chair
5. A short "platform commitment" or action plan summarizing what should happen next
